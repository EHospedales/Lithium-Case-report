Section 1: Background

The History & Science of Lithium

A Brief History of Lithium

1817

Lithium element discovered by Johan August Arfwedson in Stockholm.

1940s

Used as a sodium substitute in cardiac patients — withdrawn after fatal toxicity reports.

1949

John Cade (Australia) discovers lithium's anti-manic properties; publishes landmark paper in MJA.

1970

FDA approves lithium for treatment of acute mania.

1974

FDA approves lithium for maintenance therapy in bipolar disorder.

1990s–2000s

Growing evidence for lithium's neuroprotective and neurotrophic effects; antisuicidal properties documented.

2025

Nature publication — the article under review today. Open the paper and identify the key finding to add here as a group.

Mechanism of Action

Lithium's therapeutic effects are mediated through multiple interacting pathways.


🔬 GSK-3β Inhibition

Inhibits glycogen synthase kinase-3β → reduces tau phosphorylation, promotes neuronal survival, modulates circadian rhythm, and attenuates dopaminergic signaling.

🔄 Inositol Depletion

Blocks inositol monophosphatase → depletes free inositol → dampens IP₃ signaling cascade linked to mood instability.

🧬 Neuroprotection

Upregulates BDNF and Bcl-2 (anti-apoptotic), promotes hippocampal neurogenesis, inhibits NMDA receptor excitotoxicity.


Secondary Ion Effects

Li⁺ competes with Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, and Mg²⁺ at ion channels and transporters, affecting neuronal excitability and second-messenger systems (cAMP, PKC).

Clinical Use: Indications & Dosing

FDA-Approved Indications

  • Acute mania (bipolar I disorder)
  • Maintenance therapy — bipolar disorder

Common Off-Label Uses

  • Bipolar depression (augmentation)
  • Major depressive disorder augmentation
  • Cluster headache prophylaxis
  • Schizoaffective disorder
  • Antisuicidal therapy

Therapeutic Drug Monitoring

IndicationTarget Level (12h trough)
Acute mania0.8–1.2 mEq/L
Maintenance0.6–0.8 mEq/L
Elderly / renal impairment0.4–0.6 mEq/L
Toxic>1.5 mEq/L

⚠️ Narrow therapeutic index — levels checked 5–7 days after each dose change, then every 3–6 months when stable.

Monitoring & Safety

✅ Baseline Workup
  • BMP / serum creatinine (renal function)
  • Thyroid function tests (TSH, free T4)
  • Urinalysis
  • Pregnancy test (females of reproductive age)
  • ECG (patients >50 or cardiac history)
  • Weight / BMI
⚠️ Signs of Toxicity

Early (<1.5 mEq/L)

  • Fine tremor, polyuria, polydipsia
  • Nausea, loose stools, weight gain

Moderate (1.5–2.5 mEq/L)

  • Coarse tremor, ataxia, confusion
  • Slurred speech, lethargy

Severe (>2.5 mEq/L) — Emergency

  • Seizures, coma, cardiac arrhythmia
  • Irreversible neurological damage (SILENT)

Critical Appraisal of the Nature 2025 Article

Study Overview

The 2025 Nature article (doi:10.1038/s41586-025-09335-x) represents a significant advance in our understanding of lithium's role in psychiatric treatment.


Study Type & Design ▾

Review the paper to identify: Was this a randomized controlled trial, observational cohort, mechanistic study, or meta-analysis? The study design determines the strength of causal inference.

Population & Sample ▾

Key questions: Who was included/excluded? Was the sample representative of your patient population? Consider age, sex, comorbidities, and prior treatment history.

Primary Outcome ▾

Identify the primary endpoint. Was it clinically meaningful (e.g., relapse prevention, symptom scores, biomarker levels)? Was it pre-registered?

Intervention & Comparator ▾

What was the intervention (dose, formulation, duration)? What was the comparator — placebo, active treatment, or standard of care? Was blinding maintained?

Statistical Analysis ▾

Review: Was the study powered appropriately? Were intention-to-treat or per-protocol analyses used? How were missing data handled? Watch for multiple comparisons issues.

Funding & COI ▾

Always check: Who funded the study? Do any authors have conflicts of interest? Industry funding is associated with more favorable outcomes in trials (sponsorship bias).

Section 3: Case Discussion

Applying Evidence to Clinical Practice

Case Vignette

34-year-old woman with a 5-year history of Bipolar I Disorder presents to the ED with a 2-day history of nausea, vomiting, and hand tremor.

She has been on lithium carbonate 900 mg/day (last level 0.8 mEq/L, 6 weeks ago) and started ibuprofen 400 mg three times daily 10 days ago for a knee injury. She is otherwise well, with normal PO intake.

Vitals: BP 118/76, HR 88, T 37.1°C. Neuro exam: coarse tremor, mild confusion, ataxic gait.

What is the most appropriate next step?

❌ Incorrect – Do Not Discharge. This patient has neurological signs (coarse tremor, confusion, ataxia) consistent with moderate lithium toxicity (likely level >1.5 mEq/L). NSAID co-administration reduces renal prostaglandins → reduced renal blood flow → decreased lithium clearance. This is a medical emergency.
✅ Correct! Hold lithium immediately. Check STAT lithium level + BMP (assess renal function, electrolytes). Start IV normal saline for volume replacement to enhance renal lithium excretion. Monitor neurological status. If level >2.5 mEq/L or worsening neuro status → consider nephrology/toxicology consult for hemodialysis.
❌ Dangerous. Increasing the dose with active toxicity would be life-threatening. Confusion and tremor are signs of toxicity, not sub-therapeutic levels. Never increase lithium when the clinical picture suggests toxicity.
⚠️ Not appropriate acutely. While switching mood stabilizers may eventually be considered, this is not the priority. The immediate issue is lithium toxicity management. Do not discharge — admit for monitoring and treatment.

Clinical Implications & Take-Home Points

Practice Change Potential

  • Consider lithium earlier in bipolar disorder — not just as third-line
  • Exploit antisuicidal properties in high-risk patients
  • Screen regularly for renal and thyroid effects
  • Educate patients on hydration and NSAID avoidance
  • Monitor levels proactively with any new medication or illness
